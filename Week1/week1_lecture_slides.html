01 / 30 MSc Creative Computing — Lecture 1

Practice
as Research

Lecture 1 of 8 — Course introduction & "What is research?"
Mick Grierson — Creative Computing Institute, UAL

Set the tone — this is a course about how to think and work, not a tools course. The goal across 8 weeks is to give you a process you can keep using long after this module ends.

02 / 30Why this course exists

Why this course exists

You already know how to make things. This course is about making things that mean something to a wider conversation.

Three claims we keep returning to

  1. Finding a voice — a unique approach others can read in multiple ways.
  2. Finding a problem — locating a real gap in current understanding.
  3. The almost-perfect situation — a gap that also lets you develop a voice.

Read these slowly. Tell them: most students spend the term oscillating between (1) and (2). The A-grade work happens when (3) clicks. We'll talk about how to engineer that, not wait for inspiration.

03 / 30Two routes to the same place

Two routes to the same place

If you come from artistic practice

The work is about finding a voice — an approach, a sensibility, a way of asking questions through making.

If you come from a technical background

The work is about combining technologies, developing a new use of a technology, or co-opting one in an unexpected way.

The strongest projects do both.

Be explicit — neither route is superior. Technical students sometimes assume "art" means "decorative". Artists sometimes assume "technical" means "instrumental". Both wrong. We'll see examples this lecture where the two are inseparable.

04 / 30What you will be assessed on

What you will be assessed on (in broad terms)

I'm team-teaching, so others will set specifics. But across the field, the grading logic tends to be:

  • C / B band: a project that draws inspiration from specific works, with clear attribution and competent execution.
  • A band: a project that builds on existing work to produce a notably new or unique outcome.

Citation is the floor. Contribution is the ceiling.

Don't dwell on grading politics. The point is that the same intellectual move — situating your work against existing work — is what separates a competent project from a distinctive one. We'll spend Lecture 3 on exactly this.

The 8-week arc

WeekFocus

3Situating research in a field — finding the GAP

Weeks 1–3 are about positioning (what are you doing and why does it matter), weeks 4–6 are about doing it well (method, reflection, documentation), weeks 7–8 are about making it land (where it goes, how you frame it). Sequential for a reason.

06 / 30How to get the most out of this module

Three habits, starting today

  1. Keep a working notebook. Anything — text, sketches, links, code, voice notes. Date everything.
  2. Read one thing a week you wouldn't normally read. Outside your discipline.
  3. Make something small every week. A test, a sketch, a probe. Not a finished piece.

The students who do these three things produce the best work. Every year. Without exception.

This is the single most important slide for getting good work out of them. Be emphatic. The notebook habit becomes the documentation in week 6. The reading habit becomes the contextualisation in week 3. The weekly making becomes the methods in week 4.

07 / 30Today's session

Today's session

Three questions

  1. What is research? (the edge, the hypothesis, the test)
  2. What is the point of research? (new knowledge — and is knowledge fixed?)
  3. Who cares? (who is research for, who benefits)

Then: examples — what does this look like in technology arts?

Lay out the structure so they can follow. Tell them we'll use real examples throughout, not abstractions.

08 / 30What is research?

What is research? (a working definition)

Research is a structured attempt to produce something that wasn't known before, in a form others can examine, test, or build on.

Three components

  • The edge — where the known runs out.
  • The hypothesis — your bet about what's beyond it.
  • The test — what you'll do to find out, and how others will judge it.

This definition deliberately avoids the "scientific method" framing. The edge / hypothesis / test triad works for a lab experiment, a software prototype, an installation, or a film. General enough to cover practice-based work without being so loose it means nothing.

09 / 30The edge

"The edge"

The edge is what is not yet understood, or what is understood but unexplored, or what is assumed but not tested.

Locating the edge requires

  • knowing what is already done (Lecture 3)
  • knowing who is doing it now (Lecture 3)
  • knowing what they think is unresolved (Lecture 3)

You cannot find an edge from inside your own head.

This is the move that distinguishes a "project" from "research". A project says "I want to make X." Research says "I want to make X because the field needs Y, and X is my bet on what Y looks like." Both are valid. Only one is research.

10 / 30Project · Theme · Question

Project vs Theme vs Research Question

What it isExample
Project A thing you intend to make "I'll build a generative audio installation"
Theme A territory you're interested in "Memory, sound, and place"
Research question A specific, answerable interrogation of the territory "Can a generative audio system trained on a specific location's recordings produce material that listeners associate with that place?"

Most students arrive with a project. The work of this module is to get you to a research question.

Worth dwelling on. Ask the room: who has a project in mind? Now: what's the theme behind it? Now: what's the question? You'll get blank looks for the third column. That's the gap this module fills.

11 / 30Hypothesis

Hypothesis, in practice-based work

A hypothesis doesn't have to be a falsifiable scientific claim. In practice-based research it is more often:

  • A wager — "I think if I do this, that will happen, and it will be interesting because…"
  • A position — "I claim that current approaches miss this, and my work demonstrates an alternative."
  • A probe — "I don't yet know what happens if this, and finding out is itself the contribution."

All three are legitimate. They map onto experiment, prototype, and probe — which we'll cover next week.

Take the pressure off the word "hypothesis". For artists this word is alien. Translate it: it's just being explicit about what you think will happen and why anyone should care.

12 / 30The test

The test

What counts as a "test" in practice-based research?

  • Public exhibition — does the work read as you intended? What do audiences do with it?
  • Peer review — does the field accept the framing, the method, the contribution?
  • Critical writing — can you articulate what you did, why, and what it shows?
  • Replication / extension — can someone else build on it?

A piece of work that no-one but you can interrogate is not yet research.

It might still be art. It might still be valuable. But it is not research.

This is the bit where some artists push back. Be ready. The point is not "research is better than art". The point is "if you want it to count as research, it has to be open to scrutiny by others." Plenty of brilliant art is not research and doesn't need to be.

13 / 30What is the point?

What is the point of research?

To produce new knowledge.

But — is knowledge a fixed thing?

  • Knowledge as propositional ("X is true")
  • Knowledge as procedural ("here is how to do X")
  • Knowledge as tacit ("I know how to do X but cannot fully say how")
  • Knowledge as situated ("X is true here, for these people, in this context")

Practice-based research mostly produces the procedural, tacit, and situated kinds. This is not a deficiency — it's the point.

This slide pre-empts a lot of confusion. Students from STEM backgrounds default to propositional knowledge as "real" knowledge. They need to understand that knowing how to do something — and being able to show others — is a contribution. Smith and Dean, week 2, will give them a vocabulary for this.

14 / 30Why it matters

Why does it matter?

Research in technology arts has, in the last 25 years:

  • Shaped how we think about machine learning's creative potential — before the current AI moment.
  • Built tools that millions of artists now use (Wekinator, Magenta, RunwayML, Stable Diffusion's UI ecosystems).
  • Forced legal, ethical, and aesthetic questions onto policy agendas (copyright, training data, authorship).
  • Produced cultural artefacts that change how people understand the technology itself.

If artists weren't doing this work, someone else would be defining what these technologies are for.

This is where I tell them about my recent intervention at the Tony Blair Institute "Rebooting Copyright" launch in 2025 — challenging Microsoft Legal on model memorisation, with DSIT in the room. That's a direct line from practice-based research to policy. Their work might do the same.

15 / 30Who cares?

Who cares? Who is it for?

Research has multiple audiences. Be honest about which one(s) you're addressing:

  • Other practitioners — they want methods, tools, and demonstrations they can build on.
  • Academic peers — they want a clear contribution to a literature.
  • Audiences / publics — they want experiences that move, provoke, or illuminate.
  • Industry / policy — they want frameworks, evidence, and arguments.
  • Future you — they want documentation good enough to build on in 2 years.

Most strong projects address 2–3 of these deliberately. Weak projects address none of them and hope for the best.

Push them to name the audience early. "Who is this for?" is the question that most clarifies muddled projects. "Everyone" is not an answer.

16 / 30Some examples

Some broad examples of art-tech research

A small, partial set of examples — there are significant gaps in this list. None of this is required reading today, but it gestures at the soil this course grows from.

  • Roy Ascott — telematic art (1980s). Networks as artistic medium before the web. La Plissure du Texte (1983) explored distributed authorship across continents.
  • Nam June Paik — video synthesisers, TV Buddha, Magnet TV (1960s–70s). Co-opting consumer technology to expose its assumptions.
  • Janet Cardiff & George Bures Miller — audio walks, immersive sound installations. Research into perception, place, and narrative through built experience.
  • Rafael Lozano-Hemmer — Pulse Room, Vectorial Elevation. Public-scale interactive systems. Research into participation and crowd phenomena.

Pick one or two to dwell on depending on the room. Ascott is good because most students haven't heard of him and the work pre-dates the conditions it predicted. Paik is good for the "co-opting technology" point — directly relevant to the technical-route students.

17 / 30Why those four

Why those four?

Each of them did one specific thing this course is asking you to do:

  • Ascott — situated his practice in a theoretical conversation (cybernetics, systems theory) and produced work that advanced both the theory and the art.
  • Paik — took a mass-market technology and used it against its own grain. The work is the argument.
  • Cardiff & Miller — built rigorous methods around an experiential medium that resists rigour.
  • Lozano-Hemmer — made the engineering visible as part of the meaning, not hidden behind the spectacle.

This is the slide that connects the historical examples back to the assessment criteria. Each of these artists would score an A on this module, and you can show why.

18 / 30Marclay — The Clock

Closer to home: Christian Marclay's The Clock

24-hour single-channel video, 2010. A working clock made entirely of film clips showing the corresponding time. Winner of the Golden Lion at the 2011 Venice Biennale.

  • The edge: can found footage be reorganised so that the artwork is a functional object?
  • The method: an enormous, carefully indexed database; bespoke software for synchronisation and playback; rigorous editorial decisions.
  • The contribution: a new compositional logic for moving image — and a working argument about cinema, time, and labour.

I designed and built the playback software for the first version of The Clock. The technical work is part of the artwork, not a service to it.

Use this to make the point about technical work as research. The software wasn't generic — it had to do something nobody had needed software to do before. That's where the technical contribution sits. Mention casually; don't oversell.

19 / 30Sigur Rós — Route One

Closer to home: Sigur Rós, Route One (2016)

A 24-hour generative live broadcast: a slow drive around Iceland's Route 1, with the band's music continuously regenerated by software in real time so that no two minutes are the same.

  • The edge: can a piece of recorded music be re-imagined as a live, infinite, generative system — broadcast to a public audience?
  • The method: custom generative audio software (which I wrote), long-form camera work, public broadcast infrastructure.
  • The contribution: a working demonstration that "the album" and "the live performance" aren't the only formats music can take.

Tie this back to slide 3 — technical-route students often think generative systems are the output. Here, the generative system is the medium, and the artistic argument is about format, duration, and what counts as a "release". The technology is co-opted, not just deployed.

20 / 30MAGNet

From my own practice: MAGNet (2016)

Neural audio synthesis at CD quality, using LSTMs over STFT frames. Among the first deep learning systems to generate raw audio with musical structure.

  • The edge (then): generative audio was either symbolic (MIDI) or low-fidelity. Could deep learning produce listenable musical material?
  • The method: training neural networks on spectral representations; iterative listening tests; collaboration with composers (Robert Del Naja).
  • The contribution: a working architecture, public demonstrations (Barbican AI: More Than Human, 2019), and a body of subsequent work — including the Network Bending lineage which gave artists a vocabulary for intervening inside generative models.

Related: Memo Akten's Learning to See (2017, with Fiebrink & Grierson) and Terence Broad's Blade Runner—Autoencoded (2016) sit in the same lineage.

Be honest about scale. MAGNet was small and weird. It mattered because it opened a door. Most of your projects will be similar — small, weird, opening a door. That's enough.

21 / 30Adam Cole — Kiss/Crash

From a recent CCI student: Adam Cole, Kiss/Crash

Adam Cole's MSc work — a deeply researched immersive triptych engaging with how generative image models depict intimacy and violence, and what that says about their training distributions. Shortlisted for the Lumen Prize; XR Audience Award winner at SXSW 2024.

  • The edge: what do these models avoid, conflate, or hallucinate when asked about contact between bodies?
  • The method: systematic prompting, comparative analysis, careful framing of presentation — written up as a SIGGRAPH 2023 Art Paper.
  • The contribution: simultaneously an artwork and a critical investigation of the model's latent geometry.

This is what an A-grade outcome looks like: voice + gap, executed.

Use Adam as the proof point that this is achievable on this course, by people in the room's position 12 months ago. Don't oversell — show the work and let them respond.

22 / 30One paper, a whole map

A research lineage from one paper

Broad, Berns, Colton, Grierson (2021), Active Divergence with Generative Deep Learning (ICCC'21).

The paper surveys techniques that deliberately push generative models away from their training distribution. It cites:

  • Teratome (Broad, 2020) — network bending as artistic method.
  • You Are Here (Schultz, 2020) — chained models, custom GANs, super-resolution.
  • Network blending (Pinkney & Adler, 2020) — combining models trained on different data.
  • Network Bending (Broad, Leymarie, Grierson, 2021) — the underlying technique paper.

One good survey paper is worth a hundred Twitter threads.

Land the practical lesson hard. Survey papers are how you get oriented in a field fast. Lecture 3 will go deeper on this. For now: when you find a survey paper that maps your area, read it carefully and follow its citations.

23 / 30What people agree is worthwhile

What approaches do people agree are worthwhile?

There is genuine consensus in the field on a few things:

  • Reproducibility-where-possible. Code, data, documentation that lets others extend your work.
  • Honest negative results. "I tried X, it didn't work, here's what I learned" is publishable.
  • Open methods. The how matters as much as the what.
  • Critical reflexivity. What assumptions did you bring? What did the work surface that you didn't expect?
  • Ethical seriousness. Especially around training data, labour, consent, and representation.

These are also the criteria that get work into the venues that matter — NeurIPS Creativity workshops, ISEA, Ars Electronica, Leonardo.

Frame these as "the things a strong examiner / reviewer / curator will look for". They are not optional extras; they are the load-bearing structure of how the field judges work.

24 / 30Where there is no consensus

Where there is not consensus

Be aware these are contested:

  • What "originality" means in an era of large pre-trained models.
  • Whether AI-generated work can be authored, and by whom.
  • Whether scale (compute, data) is itself a form of contribution.
  • The relative weight of artefact vs documentation vs argument.
  • Who counts as "the field" — academia, industry, festival circuits, online communities.

You don't need to resolve these. You do need to know they exist, and to position your work knowingly within them.

This is where the critical-framing skills (Lecture 8) start. Awareness now; refinement later. Mention that I challenged Microsoft Legal on memorisation precisely because these questions are unresolved and somebody had to put a position on the table.

25 / 30A working process

A simple working process (for the rest of the term)

Every week, for your own project:

  1. One thing read — an article, paper, or work, with a paragraph of notes on what it does and what it leaves open.
  2. One thing made — a sketch, prototype, test, or fragment.
  3. One question refined — the research question you came in with, rewritten in light of (1) and (2).

Bring all three to your tutorials. By week 8 you will have 24 reads, 24 makes, and 24 versions of your question.

You will not be short of material.

This is the operational core. If they do this, the module works. If they don't, no amount of cleverness in the final write-up will save them. Repeat it.

26 / 30Failure modes

Common failure modes

In my experience, the projects that struggle do one of these:

  • All theme, no question. ("I'm interested in memory and sound.") — Becomes a mood board.
  • All project, no context. ("I'm building a synth.") — Becomes a tools demo.
  • All technique, no purpose. ("I'm using diffusion models.") — Becomes a tutorial.
  • All argument, no artefact. ("I want to critique surveillance.") — Becomes an essay.
  • All artefact, no argument. ("Here's the thing I made.") — Becomes a portfolio piece.

The good news: each of these is recoverable. We have 8 weeks.

Read these out, get a laugh of recognition. Tell them: by week 4 you'll know which failure mode is yours. Knowing is half the fix.

27 / 30Takeaways

What I want you to leave with today

  1. Research is a structured attempt to make something not previously known, in a form others can examine.
  2. Practice-based research produces procedural, tacit, and situated knowledge — which is real knowledge.
  3. The difference between a project and a research project is whether you can name the edge, the wager, and the test.
  4. Your assessment will reward citation as the floor and contribution as the ceiling.
  5. The weekly habits — read, make, refine — are the actual work of this module.

Slow down here. Read each one. Pause. These five lines are the take-away.
