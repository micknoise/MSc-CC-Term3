Practice as Research

How practice produces knowledge

Mick Grierson
Creative Computing Institute, UAL

Recap: Where We Left Off

Last week we established:

Research is a structured attempt to produce something not previously known, in a form others can examine.

Practice-based research mostly produces procedural, tacit, and situated knowledge.

The difference between a project and a research project is naming the edge, the wager, and the test.

Today: how does practice do this? What are the mechanisms?

Three Texts, Three Frames

Smith & Dean (2009) — practice and research as an iterative cycle, not two separate activities.

Candy (2006) — a distinction between practice-based and practice-led research, and what counts as evidence.

Borgdorff (2011) — a philosophical argument that artistic practice is itself a form of knowledge production.

Each gives you a different angle. None is complete alone.

Why Does This Matter?

At some point you need to answer:

What kind of knowledge is my project producing?

How do I know it's new?

How do I show someone else it's real?

Where does the knowledge live — in the artefact, the documentation, the method, or somewhere else?

If you can't answer these, you have a project but not yet a research project.

Smith & Dean: The Iterative Cyclic Web

Practice and research are not sequential. They are interleaved and mutually generative.

Practice generates questions that research cannot anticipate. Research reframes what you're doing and opens directions you wouldn't have found by making alone.

The cycle runs continuously, not once.

Unpacking the Cycle

Three nodes, not a linear sequence:

Practice / creative work

Research

Output (artefact and/or text)

Clockwise: the creative practice path. Anticlockwise: the research path. The two paths intersect and feed each other at every node.

That's what makes it a web, not a pipeline.

What This Means For You

Don't wait to finish making before you start contextualising. Read while you make. Let the reading change what you make.

Don't treat the written output as a retrospective report. It's a living document that evolves alongside the practice.

Expect your research question to change. If it doesn't, you probably aren't genuinely cycling.

Candy: Practice-Based vs Practice-Led

Practice-based: the creative artefact is the basis of the contribution to knowledge. The claim cannot be made without the artefact.

Practice-led: practice is the method that leads to new understandings, but the contribution is primarily articulated in text. The knowledge output is separable from the artefact.

Why the Distinction Matters

Not about quality — about where knowledge lives and how you demonstrate it.

Practice-based

Artefact must be accessible. You must articulate what knowledge it embodies. Your audience needs to experience the work.

Practice-led

Written output carries the primary claim. Insight is transferable. Artefact supports the argument but doesn't replace it.

Most MSc projects at CCI are practice-based. Know which one yours is.

What Counts as Evidence?

The artefact itself, presented where it can be encountered.

Documentation of process — notebooks, iterations, code, video.

Reflective commentary connecting decisions to knowledge produced.

Peer response and critical reception.

Comparative analysis with existing work.

None alone is sufficient. The strength is in their combination.

Borgdorff: Knowledge in Artistic Research

Artistic practice is not merely a vehicle for research — it is a mode of knowledge production in its own right.

Frayling's triad (1993):

Research into the arts — studying art from outside.
Research for the arts — instrumental research that serves practice.
Research through the arts — the artistic practice itself is the epistemic activity.

The third category is epistemologically legitimate and distinct.

What Makes It Research?

Three conditions:

Intent — the practitioner sets out to produce knowledge, not only to make a work.

Method — the practice follows some discernible method that can be articulated and examined.

Context — the work is situated within a relevant discourse, contributing to an ongoing conversation.

Without intent: craft. Without method: intuition. Without context: self-expression.

Knowledge That Doesn't Fit Neatly Into Words

Non-propositional — can't be fully captured in statements.

Embodied — carried in materials, processes, physical actions, perceptual experiences.

Non-discursive — communicates through forms other than language.

This doesn't make it less real or less rigorous. It means the methods of demonstrating it are different.

Putting the Three Together

Smith & Dean — maps the process clearly as a cycle.

Candy — gives practical categories for structuring a project.

Borgdorff — provides the philosophical justification.

Each has a failure mode: aimless cycling, oversimplified binary, shield against scrutiny.

Use them as lenses, not as alibis.

The Trap

"The work speaks for itself."

It doesn't. Or rather: it does, but not to everyone, and not about everything.

The artefact communicates something. But it doesn't communicate its own context, its relationship to prior work, or why its contribution matters.

Non-discursive knowledge still needs a discursive frame.

Write differently, not less.

What This Means For Your Project

Your process is part of your output. Document it from the start.

Your reading and your making should talk to each other. If your reading list never changes what you build, you're not cycling.

Know which mode you're in. Practice-based or practice-led? This determines how you structure everything.

Articulate, articulate, articulate.

Discussion / Exercise

15 minutes, in pairs or small groups:

Is your project practice-based or practice-led? (Candy)

Does it have intent, method, and context? (Borgdorff)

Where are you in the cycle? What are you neglecting? (Smith & Dean)

Be honest. Diagnosis, not performance.

Key Takeaways

Practice and research are not sequential — they cycle.

Know whether your knowledge lives in the artefact or in the text — and build accordingly.

Artistic practice can be legitimate inquiry — but only with intent, method, and context.

"The work speaks for itself" is almost always wrong. Write differently, not less.

Your process is your research. Document it now. Not later. Now.
