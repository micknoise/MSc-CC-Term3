## Slide 2 — Recap: where we are

Week 1: Research = edge + hypothesis + test.
Week 2: Practice produces knowledge — procedural, experiential, tacit, propositional, critical. The iterative cyclic web.

Today: **How do you find the edge?**

You cannot find an edge from inside your own head. You find it by reading what exists, understanding what it does, and identifying what it doesn't do.

That gap — between what exists and what doesn't yet — is where your research lives.

> **Speaker note:** Repeat the line from Lecture 1 slide 9: "You cannot find an edge from inside your own head." It bears repeating because most students try to skip this step. They want to build first and contextualise later. That sometimes works (practice-led direction), but even then you need to know the field well enough to recognise what your making has revealed. Today is about building that knowledge of the field.

## Slide 3 — What is "a field"?

A field is **the community of people working on related problems, and the body of work they have produced.**

It is not a topic. It is not a discipline. It is a living, ongoing conversation.

Your job is to enter that conversation knowing what has already been said, so you can say something that hasn't.

> **Speaker note:** The metaphor of research as a conversation is old but good. You wouldn't walk into a room of people discussing something, ignore everything they've said, and start talking about your own thing. That's what a project without context looks like. A good literature review is proof that you've been listening.

---

## Slide 4 — Fields are messy

In creative computing, your "field" is almost never a single discipline. It might include:

- A technical literature (e.g. machine learning, computer graphics, HCI)
- An artistic lineage (e.g. generative art, sound art, net art, expanded cinema)
- A critical/theoretical discourse (e.g. media theory, posthumanism, platform studies)
- A design community (e.g. interaction design, creative coding, speculative design)

You don't need to master all of these. But you do need to know which ones are relevant to your project and **where your contribution sits in relation to them**.

> **Speaker note:** Students in creative computing tend to either over-index on the technical literature (and ignore the artistic lineage) or over-index on the art world (and ignore the engineering). The strongest projects draw from at least two of these strands. Give an example: a project about generative music needs to know what's happening in ML *and* what artists have been doing with algorithmic composition since the 1950s.

## Slide 5 — The literature review is not a reading list

A common misconception: the literature review is a summary of everything you've read.

It isn't. A literature review is an **argument about the state of a field**. It says:

1. Here is what exists.
2. Here is what it does well.
3. Here is what it doesn't do, or does poorly, or hasn't yet tried.
4. Here is the gap — and my project will address it.

The review is **selective and purposeful**. You include what's relevant to your argument and omit what isn't, even if you read it.

> **Speaker note:** This distinction — between a reading list and an argument about a field — is the single biggest upgrade most students can make to their writing. A reading list says "I read these things." An argument says "these things, taken together, leave this specific opening." The former is a bibliography. The latter is scholarship.

## Slide 6 — What is a "gap"?

A gap is not just "nobody has done exactly my project before." That's trivially true of any project. A gap is a **meaningful absence** — something the field needs, or would benefit from, that doesn't yet exist.

Gaps come in several flavours:

**Method gap** — a known problem that hasn't been approached with this technique.
**Application gap** — a technique that hasn't been applied in this domain.
**Knowledge gap** — something we don't yet know or understand.
**Scale gap** — something that works at one scale but hasn't been tested at another.
**Critical gap** — a framing or assumption that hasn't been questioned.
**Audience gap** — work that exists for one community but hasn't reached another.

> **Speaker note:** Walk through each with a quick example. Method gap: "nobody has used diffusion models for live performance audio — they've been used for offline generation." Application gap: "reinforcement learning is well studied in games but rarely applied to choreographic composition." Knowledge gap: "we don't know how audiences perceive AI-generated music differently from human-composed music." Critical gap: "most work on generative art assumes a single author — what happens when authorship is distributed?" These should be quick and illustrative.

## Slide 7 — How to find a gap: practical strategies

**1. Read the "future work" sections.** Every good paper ends by saying what it didn't do. Those are gaps, pre-identified for you. Start there.

**2. Read survey papers and literature reviews.** These map a field in one document. They're the fastest way to understand what exists and where the edges are.

**3. Follow citation chains.** If Paper A cites Paper B, and Paper B cites Paper C — follow the chain. At some point the chain thins out. That's the frontier.

**4. Look at what's exhibited, not just what's published.** In creative computing, major artworks often precede papers. Check [Ars Electronica](https://ars.electronica.art/), [ISEA](https://isea-international.org/), [Lumen Prize](https://lumenprize.com/), [NeurIPS Creativity workshops](https://neuripscreativityworkshop.github.io/), transmediale, and festival archives.

**5. Talk to people.** Ask practitioners and researchers what they think is unresolved. You'd be surprised how often the answer is "nobody has tried X."

> **Speaker note:** Strategy 1 is the easiest and most underused. Literally go to the last two paragraphs of recent papers in your area and read what the authors say they didn't do. Strategy 4 is especially important in this field — there's important work that lives in festival catalogues and artist websites, not journals. Ignoring it is like doing a literature review in physics and only checking half the journals.

## Slide 8 — Where to search

**For technical/scientific literature:**
- [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/) — broad, fast, good citation counts
- [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/) — AI-assisted, good for finding related work
- [ACM Digital Library](https://dl.acm.org/) — HCI, graphics, multimedia, creativity & cognition
- [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/) — preprints in CS, AI, ML (not peer-reviewed — be aware)
- [IEEE Xplore](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/) — signal processing, computer vision, audio engineering

**For artistic/critical literature:**
- [Leonardo / MIT Press](https://direct.mit.edu/leon) — art, science, technology
- [ISEA archives](https://isea-archives.siggraph.org/) — electronic art symposium proceedings
- [Research Catalogue](https://www.researchcatalogue.net/) — artistic research, open access
- [e-flux](https://www.e-flux.com/) — contemporary art criticism
- [Rhizome](https://rhizome.org/) — net art, digital culture

**General:**
- [Connected Papers](https://www.connectedpapers.com/) — visual citation graph explorer. Extremely useful for mapping a field quickly.

> **Speaker note:** Spend a moment on Connected Papers — it's the tool most students don't know about and wish they'd found earlier. You put in one paper and it generates a visual graph of related work, clustered by similarity. It's not perfect, but it's a fast way to see the shape of a field. Also mention that arXiv is not peer-reviewed — students should use it but understand the difference.

## Slide 9 — Reading a paper: what to extract

You don't need to understand every line of every paper. For the purposes of situating your own work, you need five things:

1. **What is the research question?** (What gap does the paper claim to address?)
2. **What is the method?** (How did they investigate it?)
3. **What is the contribution?** (What new knowledge do they claim?)
4. **What are the limitations?** (What do the authors say they didn't do?)
5. **How does it relate to your project?** (Does it overlap? Does it leave something open that you could pursue?)

Write these five things down for every paper you read. One paragraph each. By the end of term you'll have a literature review almost written.

> **Speaker note:** This is a transferable skill. Whether students end up in academia, industry R&D, or independent practice, the ability to read a paper and extract its structure in five minutes is invaluable. Model it live if you have time: pick a paper the students will know (or one from Lecture 1's examples) and extract these five things in front of them.

## Slide 10 — From papers to a map of the field

Once you have notes on 10–15 papers, you can start to see the **structure** of the field:

- **Clusters:** groups of papers that share methods, questions, or communities. These are sub-fields.
- **Bridges:** papers that connect two clusters. These are often the most interesting — they bring an idea from one community to another.
- **Frontiers:** areas where the papers thin out. Few citations, recent dates, open questions. This is where gaps live.
- **Orthodoxies:** things everyone assumes but nobody tests. These are sometimes the most productive gaps of all.

Your project should sit at a **frontier** or challenge an **orthodoxy**. If it sits in the middle of a dense cluster, you're doing incremental work — fine for an MSc, but not where the exciting contributions happen.

> **Speaker note:** The metaphor of clusters, bridges, frontiers, and orthodoxies is a simplification but a useful one. If you've shown Connected Papers, point back to it — the visualisation literally shows clusters and frontiers. Orthodoxies are harder to spot because they're the water the fish swim in. Example: "most neural audio synthesis work assumes the output should sound 'realistic'" — that's an orthodoxy. Challenging it is how network bending started.

## Slide 11 — The gap statement

Once you've found a gap, you need to **state it clearly**. A good gap statement has three parts:

1. **What exists:** "Current approaches to X do Y using Z."
2. **What's missing:** "However, no existing work addresses W" / "These approaches assume A, which is not always true" / "This technique has not been applied to domain D."
3. **Why it matters:** "Addressing this gap would allow…" / "This matters because…"

This becomes the **opening move** of your written submission. It tells the reader: here is the field, here is the hole, and here is why I'm going to fill it.

> **Speaker note:** Read this structure out slowly. Then give a concrete example: "Current generative audio systems produce output offline, from text prompts, optimised for perceptual quality (exists). However, very little work explores real-time generative audio driven by live performance gesture, where latency and embodied interaction are primary constraints (what's missing). Addressing this would open generative audio to a performance context that demands fundamentally different design priorities (why it matters)." That took 30 seconds to say and it frames an entire MSc project.

## Slide 12 — A worked example: finding the gap for *Active Divergence*

Broad, Berns, Colton & Grierson (2021), [*Active Divergence with Generative Deep Learning*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.05599).

**What existed:** A large body of work on generative deep learning — GANs, VAEs, autoregressive models — optimised to produce outputs that resemble training data.

**What was missing:** No taxonomy of techniques for deliberately pushing generative models *away* from their training distributions. Artists were doing this — network bending, model blending, latent space manipulation — but nobody had mapped the space.

**Why it mattered:** Without a shared vocabulary and taxonomy, artists working in this area couldn't easily build on each other's work. The techniques were scattered across studios, blog posts, and individual papers.

The gap was **not** "nobody has bent a neural network." The gap was "nobody has systematically described and categorised the ways artists are doing this." That's a knowledge gap and a critical gap combined.

> **Speaker note:** This is a good example because students might initially think "the gap" has to be a missing technology. It doesn't. Here the gap was a missing *framework* — a way of understanding and organising work that already existed but wasn't yet legible as a coherent practice. Survey papers and taxonomies are legitimate and often highly cited contributions.

## Slide 13 — A worked example: finding the gap for *Kiss/Crash*

Adam Cole's MSc project (discussed in Lecture 1).

**What existed:** Extensive work on text-to-image generation — Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, Midjourney. Critical writing on bias in AI systems. Artistic work using generative images.

**What was missing:** Sustained artistic investigation of what these models *refuse, distort, or hallucinate* when asked to depict physical intimacy and violence between bodies. The models had been studied technically (bias benchmarks, safety filters) but not aesthetically or critically — through making.

**Why it mattered:** The models' blind spots and distortions reveal something about their training data and the cultural assumptions baked into them. An artwork that foregrounds these distortions is simultaneously an aesthetic experience and a critical investigation.

The gap was at the intersection of **critical gap** (an unexamined assumption — that these models handle bodies neutrally) and **method gap** (nobody had used systematic artistic prompting as a research method for studying model behaviour).

> **Speaker note:** This links back to Lecture 1 where we discussed Adam's work. The point here is to show how the same project, which we previously discussed as an example of good practice, can be reverse-engineered into a gap statement. Encourage students to try this with work they admire — find a project you think is strong, and work backwards to the gap it addressed.

## Slide 14 — What a gap is NOT

**"Nobody has done my exact project."**
That's not a gap. That's a description of all possible projects. A gap requires you to say what *specific absence in the field's knowledge* your project addresses.

**"I couldn't find any papers on my topic."**
That might mean there's a gap, or it might mean you searched badly. If a whole field is missing, you're probably looking in the wrong place — or your topic needs reframing.

**"This technology is new, so everything about it is a gap."**
New technologies still exist within existing conversations. Diffusion models are new, but generative art is sixty years old. The gap is not "use a new tool" — it's "use a new tool to do something the old tools couldn't, and explain what that reveals."

**"My project is interdisciplinary, so it doesn't fit any field."**
Interdisciplinary work sits at the intersection of *multiple* fields. You still need to know those fields. Being between fields is not the same as being outside them.

> **Speaker note:** These are the four most common false gap statements I see in student writing. Be direct about each one. The last point is especially important for creative computing students, who often retreat into "I'm interdisciplinary" as a way to avoid doing the literature work. Interdisciplinarity is harder, not easier — you have to read in multiple directions.

## Slide 15 — Situating practice-based work: the dual context

In practice-based research, you typically need to situate your work in **two** contexts:

**The technical context:** What tools, methods, architectures, or materials exist? What are their capabilities and limitations?

**The artistic/critical context:** What has been made? What aesthetic, conceptual, or critical territory has been explored? What hasn't?

Your gap often sits at the **intersection** — a technical capability that hasn't been explored artistically, or an artistic ambition that hasn't been enabled technically.

> **Speaker note:** This is where Lecture 2's frameworks become practical. Smith & Dean's iterative cyclic web and Borgdorff's research *in and through* the arts both imply dual contextualisation. You're not just reviewing the ML literature or just reviewing the art world — you're reviewing both and finding where they meet or fail to meet. That intersection is fertile ground.

## Slide 16 — How much reading is enough?

There's no magic number, but here are some rough benchmarks for an MSc project:

**Core papers:** 5–10 papers or works that are directly relevant to your specific project. You should know these well — their methods, contributions, and limitations.

**Contextual papers:** 10–20 additional papers or works that define the broader field your project sits within. You should know what they do, even if you don't know every detail.

**Peripheral references:** A further 10–20 that you cite for specific points — a definition, a technique, a historical precedent.

That gives you a reference list of roughly **25–50 items** for an MSc. Not all of these are academic papers — artworks, software tools, exhibitions, and technical documentation all count, provided you cite them properly.

> **Speaker note:** Students always ask "how many papers do I need to read?" This gives them a concrete answer while emphasising that quality matters more than quantity. Five deeply read core papers are worth more than fifty skimmed. Also clarify that citing an artwork is legitimate — if Memo Akten's *Learning to See* is relevant to your project, cite it. Give the URL, the date, the medium. Treat it with the same rigour you'd give a journal paper.

## Slide 17 — An exercise: reverse-engineer a gap

Pick one of the following works (or one from your own reading):

- [Memo Akten, *Learning to See*](https://www.memo.tv/works/learning-to-see/) (2017)
- [Holly Herndon, *Holly+*](https://holly.plus/) (2021)
- [Refik Anadol, *Unsupervised*](https://refikanadol.com/works/unsupervised/) (2022)
- [Ian Cheng, *BOB*](https://iancheng.com/BOB) (2018–19)

For your chosen work, write:

1. **What existed** before this work — what was the state of the field?
2. **What was missing** — what gap did this work address?
3. **Why it mattered** — what did the field gain?

Take 10 minutes now. Pick one, and draft the three-part gap statement on paper or in a note. We'll hear a couple out loud.

> **Speaker note:** This is the in-class exercise — keep it to 10–12 minutes of writing, then 3–5 minutes hearing examples. It's deliberately structured to mirror the gap statement format from slide 11. By reverse-engineering a gap for a work they admire, students practice the skill without the pressure of having to do it for their own project. Push them to be specific — "it was new" is not a gap statement; "it was the first real-time interactive system using a pix2pix network for live camera input, in a context where all previous work was offline" is.

## Slide 18 — Bringing it back to your project

For this week and next, you should be doing three things:

1. **Building your reading list.** Use the search tools from slide 8. Find at least 5 core papers or works relevant to your project. Extract the five questions from slide 9 for each.

2. **Drafting a gap statement.** Use the three-part structure from slide 11. It doesn't need to be final. It needs to exist.

3. **Refining your research question.** Your question from week 1 should now be sharper, because you know more about what already exists.

> **Speaker note:** This is where the weekly habit from Lecture 1 really starts to pay off. If they've been reading and making, they already have the raw material. If they haven't, this week is when the absence becomes painful. The gap statement is the foundation of everything that follows.
